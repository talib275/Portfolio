Blood Collection System Simulation

During my master's, I developed a discrete-event simulation using Simul8 to assess the efficiency of a hospital's blood sample collection process. The model replicated the full end-to-end workflow—from patient sample arrival through decision routing, transport via pod or driver systems, registration, analysis, and data upload. Each stage had its own logic and resource constraints. For instance, samples could be routed through either a pneumatic pod system or manually delivered by a driver, depending on system load. At registration, samples were split between haematology and chemistry analysers based on type. I configured delays, queues, and resource allocations (e.g. limited pod machines, drivers, and lab staff) to reflect realistic operational conditions. This simulation helped identify bottlenecks, test various routing scenarios, and measure performance metrics like turnaround time and analyser utilisation. It also offered clear visual outputs to support process improvement recommendations for lab efficiency.

I created nested queries and joined multiple tables—including Products, OrderDetails, and Categories—to extract meaningful insights. Mapping Well-Being: A Borough-Level Heatmap

In my free time, I explored the fundamentals of Python data visualisation by creating a heatmap of health ratings across London boroughs. I imported a public dataset, cleaned and pre-processed it using pandas, and matched the borough names to their corresponding areas on a UK shapefile. This gave me a deeper understanding of working with geospatial data and data merging techniques. The final output showed the percentage of residents reporting excellent health in each borough, revealing striking regional patterns. I used matplotlib and seaborn to style the map and define a custom color palette. This project helped me get comfortable with mapping libraries and reinforced the importance of clear, intuitive data presentation when exploring regional statistics.
